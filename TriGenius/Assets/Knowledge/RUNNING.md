# Coach-Grounding: Running — Sport-Specific Reference

**Purpose:** Running-specific reference data and pitfalls. General coaching behavior rules — data check, triage order, device-data caveats, communication standards, clinical-escalation rules, and the universal "ask before recommending" principle — are in the system prompt. This document covers what is specifically true for *running*, and should be consulted whenever running-specific recommendations, plans, or diagnoses are at stake.

---

## 1. Running-specific data to gather

Beyond the general data check (in the system prompt), pay particular attention to these running-specific factors:

- **Long-run share** of weekly volume (running has a hard upper bound; see §5)
- **Surface mix** (road vs. trail vs. treadmill) — affects pace–HR relationship
- **Footwear changes** in the last 4–8 weeks (new model, heel-drop change, carbon-plate introduction)
- **Cumulative impact load**: unlike cycling, running has a structural-tissue ceiling — bone, tendon, fascia all need adaptation time. "Feeling fit" can outrun structural readiness.

**Running-specific HR pitfall:** the pace–HR relationship is sensitive to wind, hills, soft surface, and heat. A "Z2 run" on a hilly trail at 28°C may run at Z3 HR with no fitness change. Treat as a confound, not as drift.

## 2. Stagnation triage — running-specific reference values

(General triage order is in the system prompt: Volume → Frequency → Consistency → Specificity → Recovery → Intensity distribution → Training age.)

### Volume — running reference values
Coaching reference values as rough orientation (not study thresholds; the evidence is mechanistic and observational — Fokkema 2020 for HM volume–performance association — no RCT-derived minimums exist):

- 5k ambitious: ~20 km/week
- 10k ambitious: ~30 km/week
- HM target time: ~30–35 km/week (Fokkema data: athletes > 32 km/week showed better times and less pace decline)
- HM sub-1:30: typically ~50+ km/week

These are context, not diagnosis. An athlete at 25 km/week training for an HM can still progress — but "more volume" should be seriously considered before adjusting intensity distribution.

### Frequency
1–2 running sessions/week structurally limits adaptation. 3+ is the usual minimum for progression. Multiple shorter sessions usually beat fewer long sessions for hobbyist volume (coaching consensus, not RCT-supported).

### Specificity
Is the athlete actually training at goal pace? HM training built on only 5k intervals misses race-pace adaptation. Conversely, a 5k-focused athlete doing only Z2 long runs misses the threshold and VO2max stimulus.

### Multi-sport interference
Hard bike sessions immediately before running quality? Sudden bike-volume increases? Wilson et al. 2012 meta-analysis: concurrent training interferes dose-dependently. In triathletes, **run-induced fatigue is more often the bottleneck** than bike-induced — running has higher recovery and injury cost.

## 3. "Polarized training" for runners — volume thresholds

(The general warning that polarization is not a universal first answer is in the system prompt.)

Running-specific application — as rough orientation, not study thresholds:

| Weekly volume | What matters first |
|---|---|
| < 25 km | Build volume, consistency, simple structure (1× tempo OR strides is enough) |
| 25–40 km | Easy = truly easy, 1 clear quality workout per week |
| 40–60 km | Intensity distribution becomes notably relevant, 1–2 qualities, avoid Z3 drift |
| > 60 km | Strict polarization pays off, possibly adapted Norwegian methodology |

The RCT evidence for polarization in running (Stöggl & Sperlich 2014, Muñoz 2014, Esteve-Lanao, Casado systematic review 2022) is from subjects with established volume (5+ sessions/week, several hours of training time). For very low volumes (< 25 km/week, 2–3 sessions), there is no direct evidence that polarization is the decisive lever — volume build-up and consistency plausibly dominate.

**Norwegian Double Threshold:** Plausibly not useful for recreational runners under ~50 km/week. Requires volume, recovery capacity, and ideally lactate measurement. The original applications (Ingebrigtsen et al.) are at elite level. Adapted single-threshold workouts are unproblematic at moderate volume.

## 4. The 10% rule & progression for runners

(General progression principles in the system prompt: never volume + intensity in the same week; deload every 3–4 weeks; sudden single-session spikes are the strongest injury signal.)

**Running-specific corridors:**
- Beginner: 5–10% weekly progression
- Advanced: 10–20%
- **Single long-run progression: ≤ 10% over the 30-day longest run** — single-session spike is a stronger injury predictor than weekly average
- Nielsen 2014: weekly increases > 30% notably elevate injury risk in runners

The 10% rule itself has thin direct evidence (Buist 2008 found no difference between 10% and 24% progression for injury rates in beginners; the rule's origin is "lost in history" per Foster). Use as orientation, not as law.

## 5. Volume corridors (quick reference)

### Couch → 5k (8–12 weeks)
- Start: 5–10 km/wk, 3 sessions, run-walk
- End: 15–20 km/wk, continuous 5k

### 5k → 10k (8–12 weeks)
- Base: 15 → 25 km/wk
- Build: 25 → 35 km/wk
- 3–4 sessions, 1 long run (max ~12 km), 1 quality

### 10k → HM (12–16 weeks)
- Base: 25 → 40 km/wk
- Build: 40 → 55 km/wk
- Long-run peak: 18–21 km
- 4–5 sessions

### Long run as % of weekly volume
- **Upper bound: 25–35%** — above this, injury risk rises
- Unlike cycling (where 30–40% is tolerated), running has a structural cost that hard-caps the share

### Triathlon run volume (rules of thumb)
- Sprint: 20–30 km/wk
- Olympic: 25–40 km/wk
- Middle distance (70.3): 35–55 km/wk
- Long distance (IM): 45–75 km/wk

## 6. Workout types — common misprescriptions

**Pitfall: Beginners getting VO2max intervals before the base is built.**
→ No Z5 intervals before ~20 km/week of stable base volume — injury risk outweighs adaptation gain.

**Pitfall: "Tempo run" used as a vague term.**
- Z3 (marathon pace) ≠ Z4 (threshold) — the coach must be specific
- Z3 is not "forbidden grey zone" — it has a legitimate role for HM/marathon specificity, but limited per week

**Pitfall: Easy runs run too fast.**
- If the athlete complains about the pace on an "easy run," the pace is too high
- Cap by HR or RPE; give explicit permission to slow down

**Strides — underrated:**
- 6–8 × 15–25 s submaximal, 1–2× per week
- Low injury risk, neuromuscular gains, suitable even for beginners
- The cheapest quality stimulus available; consider before more demanding quality work

**Pitfall: Quality intervals executed too hard.**
- If reps shorten or the first interval is the hardest, *time at the target intensity* drops — which is the actual stimulus
- Pace the full set to be sustainable

## 7. Strength training for runners — not optional

If the athlete does no strength training:
- **Running economy improvement: 2–8% in 8–12 weeks** (meta-analyses) — not replaceable by running alone
- **Injury risk reduced ~60–66%** (Lauersen et al. meta-analyses)
- **Minimum for effect: 2× per week, 30–45 min**
- Combination of heavy + plyometric outperforms either alone
- **Prioritize posterior chain and calves** — running-specific load demands

**Timing:**
- Run first, strength after on the same day (≥ 6 h apart)
- Plyometrics not before the long run
- No heavy strength in the 24 h before quality running

**For triathletes:** Cycling does NOT replace strength training. The loading patterns and intensity-duration profile are different stimuli.

## 8. Recovery — running specifics

(General recovery principles, REDs awareness, HRV interpretation rules, and clinical-escalation triggers are in the system prompt.)

**Running-specific recovery considerations:**
- Cumulative impact load is the dominant injury risk factor — recovery is partly tissue-level, not just systemic
- Higher frequency at moderate distances usually beats fewer long sessions for tissue adaptation
- Bone, tendon, and fascia adapt slower than the cardiovascular system → cardio readiness ≠ structural readiness
- "Niggle" management: a developing niggle is a stronger signal than HRV in runners; cross-train through it rather than push

## 9. Nutrition — running specifics

(General REDs, iron-deficiency, and clinical-escalation rules are in the system prompt.)

**Iron deficiency in female runners:**
- 15–35% prevalence
- Ferritin < 30 ng/mL often considered relevant for athletes (some sources < 50)
- Symptoms: unexplained fatigue, performance drop, breathlessness at usual paces
- **Woman + stagnation + fatigue → always recommend a ferritin check via physician**

**Carbohydrate intake during running (rules of thumb):**
- < 1 h: nothing required
- 1–2 h: 30–60 g/h
- > 2 h: 60–90 g/h, ideally 2:1 glucose:fructose (multiple-transportable-carbs)
- > 2.5 h with trained gut: up to 90–120 g/h (Podlogar et al. 2022; treat 90 g/h as conservative ceiling, 120 g/h as aspirational for race-trained athletes)
- **The gut must be trained** — 2–4 weeks of long-run sessions practicing race-level carb intake
- **Running tolerates less than cycling** due to GI jostling. In triathlon, the bike leg is the primary fueling window for the entire race.

**LCHF/Keto for running performance:** Evidence speaks clearly against it for intensities above moderate aerobic. Do not recommend for performance-focused runners.

## 10. Tapering — quick reference

- Duration: 7–21 days, optimum typically **8–14 days**
- Volume: −40 to −60%
- **Intensity MAINTAINED** (cutting intensity is detraining, not tapering)
- Frequency: ≤ 20% reduction
- Progressive (exponentially declining) slightly better than step
- Expected performance gain: 0.5–6% (Bosquet meta-analysis)

## 11. Triathlon specifics for the run

### Run off the bike
- First 400–700 m biomechanically unstable — no quality pace work there
- Energy cost +2–11% after the bike (depends on training status)
- Brick workouts: 1× per week in build/peak phases

### Interference (Wilson et al. 2012)
- Bike-hard and run-hard on different days, or ≥ 6 h apart
- Long bike and long run separated by ≥ 24–48 h
- Swimming barely interferes with running

### Run frequency beats distance per session
- 4 × 8 km > 2 × 16 km for most age-group triathletes — less impact per session, more adaptation cycles, lower injury cost

### Common brick mistake
- Every long bike followed by a long run multiplies fatigue and run injury risk. Most bricks should have a *short* run (10–20 min) if the goal is transition adaptation. Long-run-after-long-ride is for peak specificity only, and sparingly.

## 12. Schrittfrequenz (Cadence)

**Grundlage:** Geschwindigkeit = Kadenz × Schrittlänge. Beide Stellschrauben sind austauschbar — es gibt keine physiologisch "korrekte" Kadenz, sondern individuelle Optima, die von Beinlänge, Tempo und Lauferfahrung abhängen.

**Die "180 spm"-Regel (Jack Daniels) ist kein wissenschaftlicher Grenzwert.** Sie basiert auf einer informellen Beobachtung bei Elite-Läufern der Olympischen Spiele 1984, nicht auf einer kontrollierten Studie. Sie überträgt sich nicht 1:1 auf Freizeitläufer, kleinere Menschen oder langsamere Tempi. Als grobe Orientierung, nicht als Ziel für jeden Athleten.

### Typische Kadenz-Bereiche (grobe Orientierung, keine Studien-Schwellenwerte)

| Läufertyp / Tempo | Kadenz (spm) |
|---|---|
| Anfänger, Easy Pace | 150–165 | 
| Freizeitläufer, Easy Pace | 160–175 |
| Freizeitläufer, Race Pace (10k–HM) | 170–185 |
| Elite, Easy Pace | 175–185 |
| Elite, Race Pace | 185–200+ |

Kadenz steigt mit Tempo (bis zu einem Plateau) und tendenziell mit Lauferfahrung. Große Menschen laufen bei gleichem Tempo oft mit etwas niedrigerer Kadenz als kleine — das ist kein Fehler.

**Overstriding ist das eigentliche Problem, nicht niedrige Kadenz per se.** Overstriding = Aufsatzpunkt des Fußes deutlich vor dem Körperschwerpunkt, meist mit gestrecktem Knie. Das erhöht Bremskräfte und vertikale Belastungsraten. Eine niedrige Kadenz korreliert oft mit Overstriding, ist aber nicht dasselbe — ein Läufer kann niedrige Kadenz und trotzdem sauberen Fußaufsatz haben.

**Evidenz zu Kadenzerhöhung und Verletzungsrisiko (moderat, mechanistisch fundiert, nicht durch große RCTs auf harte Endpunkte bestätigt):**
- Eine Erhöhung der Kadenz um **5–10 %** gegenüber der selbstgewählten Kadenz reduziert nachweislich vertikale Belastungsspitzen, Bremskräfte und die Belastung am Knie (patellofemoral) sowie an der Hüfte (Heiderscheit et al. 2011).
- Mehr als 10 % Erhöhung bringt keinen klaren Zusatznutzen und erhöht die metabolische Kosten sowie die Belastung am Sprunggelenk/der Achillessehne.
- Assoziative Daten (Luedke 2016, Kliethermes 2021) verknüpfen niedrigere Kadenz mit erhöhtem Risiko für tibiale Stressreaktionen — Kausalität nicht durch RCT belegt.

### Pitfall: Kadenz-Coaching bei beschwerdefreien Läufern
Ein Läufer ohne Beschwerden, ohne Overstriding-Muster und mit stabiler Leistungsentwicklung braucht **keine** Kadenz-Intervention, nur weil die Uhr 165 spm statt 180 anzeigt. Kadenz ist ein Mittel, kein Ziel.

### Pitfall: Zu schnelle Kadenz-Änderung
Wie bei Volumen gilt: abrupte Technikänderungen sind ein Belastungsreiz für andere Gewebestrukturen (Waden, Achillessehne). Kadenzerhöhung schrittweise vornehmen — **max. 5–10 % pro Anpassungsphase**, über mehrere Wochen eingeschliffen (z. B. per Metronom in kurzen Intervallen von 5–10 Min. innerhalb eines Easy Runs), nicht als Dauerzustand von Tag 1 an.

### Wann Kadenz-Arbeit sinnvoll ist
- Klares Overstriding-Muster (visuell oder via Wearable: hohe Vertical Oscillation + niedrige Kadenz + langer Ground Contact Time)
- Historie von Schienbein-/Knie-Beschwerden ohne andere klare Ursache
- Explizite Laufökonomie-Optimierung bei ambitionierten Athleten

### Wann eher nicht
- Beschwerdefreier Läufer, stabile Progression
- Anfänger in den ersten 8–12 Wochen: Fokus liegt auf Volumenaufbau und Laufkonsistenz (siehe §5) — Technikfeedback zusätzlich zur Belastungssteigerung erhöht die Zahl gleichzeitiger Stressoren unnötig
- Wearable-Kadenzwert allein, ohne Beschwerden oder Bewegungsauffälligkeit, ist kein Interventionsgrund

**Anfänger-spezifisch:** Laufanfänger zeigen oft niedrigere Kadenz (150–165 spm) durch Unsicherheit, Rumpfkraft-Defizite und geringere Schrittfrequenz-Ökonomie — das ist in der Aufbauphase normal und muss nicht aktiv korrigiert werden, sofern kein Overstriding oder Beschwerden vorliegen. Metronom-Feedback kann helfen, ist aber ein zusätzlicher kognitiver Load und sollte nicht mit Volumenaufbau in derselben Trainingswoche kombiniert werden.
