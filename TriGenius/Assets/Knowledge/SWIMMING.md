# Coach-Grounding: Swimming — Sport-Specific Reference

**Purpose:** Swimming-specific reference data and pitfalls. General coaching behavior rules — data check, triage order, generic device-data caveats, communication standards, clinical-escalation rules, and the universal "ask before recommending" principle — are in the system prompt. This document covers what is specifically true for *swimming*, which differs from running and cycling in important ways.

---

## 1. The crucial swim-specific insight: the lever hierarchy is INVERTED

Unlike running and cycling, swimming is a **technique-dominated discipline** for adult-onset triathletes. The expected return-on-investment order is:

1. **Technique / propelling economy** (highest leverage)
2. **Frequency** (motor-skill consolidation)
3. **Volume** (aerobic base)
4. **Intensity mix** (smallest lever until 1–3 are addressed)

This **inverts the standard stagnation triage from the system prompt.** Before applying the general Volume → Frequency → Consistency → Specificity → Intensity order to a swim plateau, check technique first.

**Why quantitatively:**
- Triathlon swimmers have markedly lower propelling efficiency than competitive pool swimmers (Toussaint 1990)
- Drag efficiency in swimming is 0.03–0.09 — under 10% of metabolic input becomes useful mechanical work (Zamparo, Cortesi & Gatta 2020)
- Small economy gains produce disproportionately large pace gains
- 4×60 min/week beats 2×120 min/week for adult-onset athletes (motor-learning consensus)

## 2. Swimming-specific data to gather

Beyond the general data check:
- **Current CSS or T-pace** and when last tested (see §3)
- **Sessions/week, average distance, % freestyle vs. drills**
- **Adult-onset vs. lifelong swimmer** (years of structured swimming, not just "I swim")
- **Equipment**: pull buoy, paddles (size and weekly volume!), fins, snorkel
- **Pool vs. OW access** mix
- **Shoulder pain or impingement history** — especially with paddle use
- **Wetsuit fit** (for OW racers)

---

## 3. Critical Swim Speed (CSS) — the primary pacing anchor

HR is unreliable in water (§4.1). Use **pace, anchored to CSS**.

**Formula (Wakayoshi 1992):** CSS = (400 − 200) m / (T400 − T200) s.

Originally described as "indefinitely sustainable," but Dekerle et al. 2010 showed CSS is sustainable ~14–40 min — closer to MLSS / lactate threshold. **CSS ≈ low-Z4 / threshold boundary.**

**Practical use:**
- Retest every 4–8 weeks via fresh 400 m + 200 m TTs (≥10 min rest between)
- > 2% improvement in CSS pace is meaningful
- CSS sets: 8–12 × 100 @ CSS / 10–20 s rest; 5×200 @ CSS; 4–6 × 400 @ CSS +2–3 s/100
- Workouts prescribed in Z3 / Z4 must translate to CSS-anchored paces, not in-water HR

## 4. Swimming-specific device pitfalls

(General device-data caveats are in the system prompt.)

### 4.1 HR underwater — CRITICAL

- **Swim peak HR is ~7–15 bpm lower than land HR** at matched effort (Hauber, Sharp & Franke 1997; Holmér et al. 1974). This is physiological — hydrostatic pressure, prone position, cooling, dive reflex — not measurement error.
- **Wrist-optical HR is unreliable in water**; most don't record during swims. Chest straps lose contact and pick up motion artifact.
- **Do NOT apply land HR zones to swim sessions.** Garmin's Z3/Z4 in the pool is meaningless without manual recalibration.
- HRV must be measured on **land, in the morning** — never in-water.
- If in-pool HR data is implausibly low or noisy: **ignore it.** Use CSS-pace and RPE.

### 4.2 SWOLF — useful but easily misread

SWOLF = pool-length time (s) + stroke count.

**Valid when** stroke, pool length, and push-off are held constant; comparison is within-session or against matched prior sessions.

**Misleading when:**
- Compared across different pool lengths (25 m vs. 50 m differ ~25–35%)
- Compared across drills, kicks, pulls, or stroke types (Garmin aggregates them)
- Used as a sole "good swimmer" marker — over-gliding produces low SWOLF with dead-spots
- Trivially improved by longer underwater push-offs

**Useful pattern:** rising SWOLF across a constant-pace set signals fatigue.

### 4.3 Stroke metrics
- Velocity = stroke rate × stroke length
- **SL ↑ at constant velocity = efficiency gain** (the target for adult-onset)
- DPS (distance per stroke) tracks the same as SL
- **In-set pace decoupling** (1st half vs. 2nd half of a constant-effort set) is the most useful in-pool aerobic-base proxy — HR-decoupling underwater is unreliable

## 5. Stagnation triage — swim-specific override

(General triage order in the system prompt.)

**For swimming, technique is the #1 check, ahead of volume** — see §1.

Order of checks for a swim plateau:
1. **Technique limitation?** Most common in adult-onset. Indicators: high SR with low SL, SWOLF > 45 in 25 m pool, visible scissor kick, head position, poor body line. → Drill-rich lower-volume work; video analysis if available.
2. **Frequency < 3 sessions/week?** Motor-skill consolidation requires regularity.
3. **Volume below distance goal range?** See §6.
4. **Grey-zone Z3 overload?** Common in self-coached swimmers. Fix: more Z1–Z2 base + sharper Z4–Z5 blocks.
5. **Specificity missing?** No OW work / sighting / drafting / wetsuit practice before an OW race.
6. **Recovery / sleep / REDs / iron** — see system prompt; swimmers are an **elevated REDs risk group** (lean-pressure, high volumes).
7. **Equipment confounders?** Goggle leak (sighting drift), wetsuit too tight (shoulder restriction), paddle size too large.
8. **Training-age plateau?** Elite swimmers gain ~0.6–1.0% per year (Pyne 2004); adult-onset gains slow more gradually because the technique ceiling is far away.

## 6. Training intensity distribution for swimmers

(General polarization caveat is in the system prompt.)

**Swimming-specific evidence:**
- Elite swimmers train **pyramidal in base, polarized in peak/race-prep** (Hellard 2019, 20-yr French national-team cohort)
- Pla 2019 crossover (elite juniors): 6-wk polarized produced ~1% greater 100 m gain than threshold-dominated, with less fatigue
- González-Ravé 2021 systematic review: sprint → polarized/threshold; middle-distance → threshold/pyramidal; long-distance → pyramidal

**The 80/20 rule is NOT validated for swimming.** Seiler & Tønnessen (2009) derived it from runners, skiers, rowers, cyclists. Elite swimmers spend 15–30% in moderate-intensity (Z3/threshold), inconsistent with strict 80/20.

**Practical default for adult-onset triathletes:**
- Base block: pyramidal — ~65–75% Z1–Z2, 15–25% Z3, 5–10% Z4–Z5
- Build/peak (6–8 weeks pre-race): shift toward polarized — more Z1 + Z5, less Z3

## 7. Volume corridors (quick reference)

### Adult-onset starting points
- Non-swimmer → 400 m continuous: 6–10 weeks, 2–3 short sessions/week (~800–1500 m), technique-dominant
- Beginner triathlete (sprint/Olympic): 2–3 sessions/week, 1500–2500 m/session

### Finish vs. race competitively
| Distance | Finish safely | Race competitively |
|---|---|---|
| Sprint (750 m) | 3000–4000 m/wk, 2 sessions | 6000–10000 m/wk, 3–4 sessions |
| Olympic (1500 m) | 5000–6000 m/wk, 2–3 sessions | 8000–15000 m/wk, 3–4 sessions |
| 70.3 (1900 m) | 6000–8000 m/wk, 3 sessions | 10000–18000 m/wk, 4–5 sessions |
| IM (3800 m) | 8000–12000 m/wk, 3 sessions | 15000–25000 m/wk, 5–6 sessions |

Practitioner consensus values; direct RCT evidence is sparse. Elite reference: Hellard 2019 — French national team up to ~60 km/wk.

### Swim share of triathlon volume (by time)
| | Sprint | Olympic | 70.3 | IM |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Swim % | 15–25% | 15–20% | 10–15% | 8–12% |

**Frequency must remain ≥ 3×/week for skill retention regardless of distance** — even if total swim time is small.

### Progression
- ≤ 10–15% weekly increase over 3-week blocks, then deload (heuristic; no swim-specific RCT)
- After ≥ 10-day break: restart at 60–70%, re-progress over 2–3 weeks
- Costill 1991: doubling daily volume (5000 → 9300 m/d) produced no further sprint gain — diminishing returns are real

## 8. Workout types — swim-specific

**Aerobic base (Z2):** 1500–3000 m continuous or long broken (e.g., 6×500 @ CSS +8–12 s/100, short rest). Mitochondrial/capillary adaptation; technique under fatigue.

**CSS / threshold (Z3–Z4):** 1–2× per week base, 2–3× peak. Formats:
- 10–16 × 100 @ CSS, 10–20 s rest
- 5–8 × 200 @ CSS to CSS +2 s/100, 15–20 s rest
- 4–6 × 400 @ CSS +2–3 s/100, 20–30 s rest

**VO2max (Z5):** 8 × 100 maximal (~1:1 rest), 4–6 × 200 @ 95–100% race pace. 50–60 s work duration is the most-studied swim format.

**Sprint / neuromuscular:** 8–12 × 25 m all-out (2:1 rest), 6–10 × 50 m @ 200 m pace. Important for sprint-triathlon starts and surges.

**Technique / drills / sculling:** **≥ 20% of session content for adult-onset triathletes.** Targets: head position, body line, catch, hip rotation, exhalation. Short sets (4–8 × 25–50 m) with frequent focus rotation.

**Kick:** contributes only ~10–15% of front-crawl propulsion in adult-onset triathletes — excessive isolated kick volume has poor triathlon transfer. Secondary priority.

**Pull sets / pull buoy:** technique focus and wetsuit-buoyancy mimicry; **lower shoulder load than paddles** and a safer way to overload upper-body. See §9 for paddle caveats.

## 9. Strength training for swimmers — including paddle caveat

(General strength-training principles in the system prompt.)

**Swim-specific evidence:**
- Crowley, Harrison & Lyons 2017 meta-analysis: resistance training improves swim performance — strongest for sprints, starts, and turns; modest for middle/long distance
- Lauersen 2018: ~66% injury-risk reduction from strength training generally — particularly relevant given shoulder prevalence
- Effects diminish in elite trained swimmers, but adult-onset triathletes are far from that ceiling

**Format:** Heavy strength (3–5 reps, 80–90% 1RM) and explosive/plyometric both transfer. **Rotator cuff, serratus, lower trap, and scapular-stability work is mandatory** given the 40–91% shoulder-impingement prevalence in competitive swimmers (Sein 2010). 2× per week, 30–45 min, ≥ 6 h from key swim sessions.

### Paddles — IMPORTANT CAVEAT

**Paddles are the single most-cited shoulder overuse risk factor in triathletes** (Hill et al. 2018: 24% overuse rate with paddles vs. 10% without).

- Limit to < 10–15% of swim training volume
- Small-to-medium paddle size only — large "S-paddles" or "Tech Paddles" increase risk
- **Never use with existing shoulder impingement or pain**
- Parachutes and bands are alternatives with lower shoulder cost
- **Shoulder pain on stroke → cut paddles immediately**, reduce swim volume −30% for 2 weeks, foreground rotator-cuff and scapular-stability work, refer for assessment. Do not "swim through" shoulder pain.

## 10. Recovery & nutrition — swim specifics

(General recovery, REDs, iron, and CHO-intake rules are in the system prompt. Swimmers are an **elevated REDs risk group**.)

**Recovery specifics:**
- **HRV measured on land only** (in-pool HR not usable)
- **Chlorine exposure** (Bougault 2009, 2012): airway hyperresponsiveness, exercise-induced bronchoconstriction, and rhinitis are more prevalent in swimmers. Persistent cough or rhinitis → recommend EIB screening / medical referral.
- Easy swimming is a **low-impact recovery modality** between hard run sessions

**Nutrition specifics:**
- **No bottle on the swim** → in-session fueling is impractical except in very long OW events
- Pre-session: 1–2 g/kg CHO 2–3 h before; optional top-up 30 g 15 min before
- Race-day: gel taped to wetsuit or mouth-feed in transition
- Iron stress is lower than in runners (less foot-strike hemolysis), but general iron-deficiency vigilance still applies — woman + stagnation + fatigue → ferritin check

## 11. Tapering — swim reference

(General tapering principles in the system prompt.)

- Duration: **~2 weeks** (Bosquet 2007 meta-analysis)
- Volume: **−41 to −60% exponentially**; intensity unchanged; frequency ≤ 20% reduction
- Swim-specific gain: **0.9–3.5%** (Mujika 1995; Houmard 1994; Amara 2021)
- Maintain short race-pace efforts ("sharpeners") through the final 7 days
- Pre-taper overload week amplifies the gain (Mujika)

## 12. Triathlon-specific swim considerations

### Open water vs. pool
- OW economy can be 5–15% worse than pool at the same fitness if untrained
- Recommend ≥ 4–6 OW sessions in the 6–8 weeks before a key OW race

### Drafting (legal in triathlon swim)
- Chatard et al. 1998: drafting is **~3% faster** over 400 m for trained triathletes
- Passive drag reduced 13–26%
- Effective draft: < 0.5 m behind feet, or hip-position alongside
- Subsequent cycling performance is preserved or improved
- **Practice drafting before race day** — it's a free legal speed gain

### Wetsuit
- Active drag reduced 12–14% (Toussaint 1989); 400 m time reduced ~5%, 1500 m time ~3% (Chatard 1995)
- Lean triathletes benefit more (smaller buoyancy substitution effect)
- **Poor wetsuit fit** → shoulder restriction and over-rotation — a common underdiagnosed economy issue. Always check fit, especially shoulder mobility, before race day.

### Pacing the swim for the bike
- Peeling et al. 2005: higher swim intensity impairs subsequent cycling, particularly in non-drafting events
- Target: race pace ~80–90% of pool CSS for non-drafting; closer to CSS for drafting / short formats

### Interference
- Swim → bike/run: minimal (non-weight-bearing)
- Bike/run → swim: minor; primarily systemic fatigue
- **Schedule key swim sessions on fresh days** — adult-onset swimmers cannot rehearse stroke quality under heavy systemic fatigue

## 13. Common false-positive advice — flag and refuse

| Common claim | Verdict |
|---|---|
| "Just swim more — volume is king" | WRONG for adult-onset; technique > frequency > volume > intensity |
| "Always breathe bilaterally" | Weak / individual; many elite OW swimmers breathe unilaterally. Train bilateral for adaptability; race the pattern that holds pace. |
| "Hypoxic / controlled-frequency sets build VO2max" | NOT SUPPORTED (Lavin 2015; Woorons 2014, 2016; Zapata & Olcina 2020). Small gains attributable to CO2 tolerance, not hypoxic adaptation. Do not prescribe as a performance lever. |
| "Low SWOLF = good swimmer" | Misleading without stroke / pool-length / push-off context |
| "Use big paddles for strength" | OVERUSE RISK; small-medium only, low volume only |
| "Match HR zones across sports" | WRONG; swim HR ~7–15 bpm lower at matched effort |
| "Bilateral kick volume builds endurance" | Low transfer to triathlon; secondary priority |
| "Drafting is unsporting" | LEGAL in triathlon swim; ~3% faster |
| "Sight every 6–8 strokes" | Convention, not evidence — minimize sighting to what maintains line |
