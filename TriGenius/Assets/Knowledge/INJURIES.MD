# Triathlon Injury Reference for an AI Coaching Agent

**TL;DR**
- Overuse problems dominate triathlon training: Andersen/Clarsen et al. (2013, *BJSM*) found a 56% (95% CI 51–61) average prevalence of overuse complaints across iron-distance triathletes over 26 weeks; Rhind et al. (2022, *J Hum Kinet*) systematic review of short-course triathletes reported overuse-onset injuries of 50–75%. These problems concentrate in three regions — the runner's knee/lower leg, the cyclist's knee/back/perineum, and the swimmer's shoulder — and most trace back to one of four modifiable upstream factors: hip/pelvis control, ankle dorsiflexion mobility, thoracic mobility/scapular control, or bike fit.
- The single most important coach competency is **escalation triage**: differentiating low-risk overuse pain (e.g., MTSS, mid-portion Achilles, PFPS) that can be load-managed from high-risk presentations (femoral neck or anterior-cortex tibial stress fractures, cauda equina, iliac artery endofibrosis, cardiac symptoms) that require same-week medical workup.
- Evidence quality is uneven. Strong: hip-strength deficits → ITBS/PFPS (Fredericson 2000; Noehren 2007; Neal 2016); eccentric loading for mid-portion Achilles tendinopathy (Alfredson 1998); 5–10% cadence increases reduce knee joint loading (Heiderscheit 2011); strength training cuts overuse injury risk to ~half (Lauersen 2014, *BJSM*, RR 0.527, 95% CI 0.373–0.746 — "overuse injuries could be almost halved"). Weak/contested: footstrike pattern, pronation-based shoe prescription, static stretching for prevention, "core stability" as panacea.

## Key Findings

1. **Knee and shin dominate running injury volume.** Across systematic reviews (Lopes 2012; Kakouris 2021, *J Sport Health Sci*), prevalence ranks are PFPS ~17%, MTSS ~9%, plantar fasciitis ~8%, ITBS ~8%, Achilles tendinopathy ~7–10%. Novice runners suffer ~17.8 injuries/1000h vs. ~7.7 in recreational runners (Videbæk 2015, *Sports Med* meta-analysis).
2. **Cycling overuse concentrates at knee, lower back, neck, perineum, and hands.** Clarsen (2010, *Am J Sports Med*) in 109 professional road cyclists: 45% of overuse injuries lower back, 23% knee; 57% of time-loss injuries involved the knee. In recreational cyclists, anterior knee pain is the single most common gradual-onset injury (du Toit 2020, SAFER XIII, 14.2% of GOIs).
3. **Swimmer's shoulder is umbrella terminology.** Prevalence of shoulder pain in competitive swimmers is 40–91% depending on definition (Hill 2015; Struyf 2017 *BJSM*). Elite swimmers complete ~30,000 shoulder revolutions per week — volume is the dominant driver.
4. **A small number of biomechanical chains explain most variance.** Weak/uncoordinated hip abductors → contralateral pelvic drop → dynamic knee valgus → simultaneous loading of lateral knee (ITB), patellofemoral joint, and medial tibia. Limited ankle dorsiflexion increases knee valgus and ground-reaction forces. Thoracic kyphosis reduces subacromial space and is associated with scapular dyskinesis (Kibler 2013, *BJSM*).
5. **A handful of presentations require same-week referral**, not load management: femoral neck (tension-side), anterior tibial cortex ("dreaded black line"), sacral, navicular, and proximal 5th metatarsal stress fractures (Boden & Osbahr 2000, *JAAOS*); cauda equina; exertional chest pain/syncope; calf DVT signs; iliac artery endofibrosis in cyclists with unilateral high-intensity claudication.

## RUNNING INJURIES

### Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (PFPS / "Runner's Knee")
**Presentation:** Diffuse anterior or peripatellar pain, worse with descending stairs, prolonged sitting ("theatre sign"), squatting, and during/after running. Often bilateral, insidious onset.
**Prevalence:** ~17% point-prevalence in runners; ~22/1000 person-year incidence; ~62% female (Petersen 2014, *Knee Surg Sports Traumatol Arthrosc*).
**Mechanism:** Elevated patellofemoral joint stress from quadriceps weakness/imbalance, VMO timing, and proximal kinematic factors.
**Biomechanical chain:** Neal 2016 (*Gait Posture*) systematic review — moderate evidence that increased peak hip adduction, hip internal rotation, and contralateral pelvic drop are associated with PFP; limited prospective evidence that peak hip adduction predicts PFP in female runners. Gait retraining reducing peak hip adduction reduces symptoms. Higher vertical loading rates implicated; Chan 2018 prospective RCT showed "running softer" reduced PFP incidence.
**Differential (anterior knee pain in runners/cyclists):**
- Patellar tendinopathy — point tenderness at inferior patella pole, pain with jumping/decelerating
- Quadriceps tendinopathy — tenderness at superior pole
- Fat pad (Hoffa's) impingement — pain inferior to patella in terminal extension
- Plica syndrome — medial snapping, often post-trauma
- Patellofemoral OA in older athletes — crepitus
**Red flags:** Locking, true giving way, effusion (suggest internal derangement, not PFPS).
**Key references:** Crossley 2016 (*BJSM*, consensus); Willy & Meira 2016 (*JOSPT*).

### Iliotibial Band Syndrome (ITBS)
**Presentation:** Sharp/burning pain over the lateral femoral epicondyle, reproducible at ~30° knee flexion, predictably reappears at a given distance into runs.
**Prevalence:** ~7–14% of running injuries; common cause of lateral knee pain in cyclists.
**Mechanism (modern):** Compression of richly innervated fat pad deep to the ITB at the lateral femoral epicondyle, not friction (Fairclough 2006, *J Anat*; Willy 2019). The ITB is anatomically tethered to the femur — it does not "roll over" the epicondyle.
**Biomechanical chain:** Fredericson 2000 (*Clin J Sport Med*) — distance runners with ITBS had 20–30% weaker hip abductors on the injured side. Noehren 2007 (*Clin Biomech*) prospective — runners who developed ITBS had greater hip adduction and knee internal rotation. Aderem & Louw 2015 systematic review (*BMC Musculoskelet Disord*) — hip abductor endurance may be more discriminating than peak strength.
**Differential:** Lateral meniscus pathology, biceps femoris insertional tendinopathy, popliteus tendinopathy, superior tibiofibular joint dysfunction, common peroneal nerve referral, lateral patellar facet PFPS.
**Cycling-specific:** Saddle too high or too far back forces minimum knee flexion <30°, keeping ITB in compression zone; excessive cleat float, varus wedging.

### Achilles Tendinopathy (Mid-portion vs. Insertional)
**Mid-portion:** Pain/thickening 2–7 cm proximal to calcaneus. Tensile pathology. Morning stiffness; warms up then returns post-exercise.
**Insertional:** Pain at tendon-bone junction (within 2 cm of calcaneus). Compression-driven (tendon compresses against calcaneus in dorsiflexion). Associated with Haglund's deformity, retrocalcaneal bursitis.
**Prevalence:** ~7–10% of running injuries; lifetime incidence ~50% in runners (Kujala 2005, *Clin J Sport Med*).
**Why the distinction matters:** Treatments diverge sharply. Alfredson 1998 eccentric heel drops (drop heel below step level) are evidence-based for mid-portion but can worsen insertional disease by adding compression. For insertional: dorsiflexion-limited loading (heel raises stop at floor level), often with heel lifts.
**Contributors:** Reduced ankle dorsiflexion, calf-soleus stiffness, abrupt training increases, hill running, minimalist-shoe transition.
**Differential:** Posterior ankle impingement, retrocalcaneal bursitis, partial tear, posterior tibial tendinopathy (medial), peroneal tendinopathy (lateral), plantaris tendinopathy, Haglund's.
**Red flag:** Audible "pop" with inability to plantarflex → suspect rupture; Thompson test, urgent referral.

### Plantar Fasciitis / Fasciopathy
**Presentation:** Sharp heel pain with first steps in morning or after sitting; tenderness at medial calcaneal tubercle.
**Prevalence in runners:** ~6–8% of injuries.
**Risk factors:** Hamstra-Wright 2021 (*Am J Sports Med*) meta-analysis — higher BMI, increased plantarflexion ROM. Plesek 2025 (*Med Sci Sports Exerc*) — runners doing >40 km/wk had ~6× the odds vs. 6–20 km/wk; greater maximal ankle eversion associated with PF. Martin 2014 *JOSPT* CPG — limited dorsiflexion is a moderate-evidence risk factor.
**Chain:** Reduced first-MTP extension impairs windlass mechanism; calf-soleus tightness limits DF; overpronation lengthens fascia; cavus foot impairs shock absorption.
**Differential:** Calcaneal stress fracture (squeeze test positive, more diffuse), Baxter's nerve entrapment, tarsal tunnel, fat pad atrophy, fascia rupture (acute, bruising), S1 radiculopathy.
**Red flag:** Bilateral heel pain in young male → consider seronegative spondyloarthropathy.

### Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome (MTSS)
**Presentation:** Diffuse pain along distal two-thirds of posteromedial tibial border, tenderness >5 cm in length, worse with running, improves with rest.
**Prevalence:** 9–18% of running injuries; higher in females and novices.
**Mechanism:** Bony overload at posteromedial tibial cortex; histologically part of the same bone stress continuum as tibial stress fractures (Moen 2009, *Sports Med*).
**Chain:** Increased navicular drop, reduced hip external rotation strength, increased BMI, prior MTSS, female sex, hard surfaces. Lower cadence prospectively predictive (Luedke 2016).
**Differential (medial shin pain — critical for triage):**
- **Tibial stress fracture:** focal tenderness (<5 cm), crescendo pain, positive hop test, night pain; anterior cortex variant is high-risk ("dreaded black line").
- **CECS:** predictable crampy pain building over 10–30 min, resolves with rest; may have paresthesia/foot drop. Pedowitz criteria for intracompartmental pressure (>30 mmHg at 1 min post-exercise).
- **Tibialis posterior tendinopathy:** pain posterior to medial malleolus, worse with single-leg heel raise.
- **Popliteal artery entrapment:** abnormal pulses with active plantarflexion.
- **L4–S1 radiculopathy:** distal symptoms, neuro signs.
**Red flags:** Crescendo pain, point tenderness, night pain, female with menstrual disturbance → image for BSI.

### Bone Stress Injuries — Framework
**Spectrum:** Stress reaction (Fredericson MRI grades 1–2: periosteal/marrow edema, no fracture line) → stress fracture (grades 3–4a: intracortical change) → complete fracture (grade 4b: linear cortical line). Fredericson 1995 (*Am J Sports Med*) original tibial classification; correlates with return-to-sport time. Kaeding & Miller 2013 (*JBJS*) 5-grade comprehensive system (I asymptomatic stress reaction; II symptomatic, no fracture line; III non-displaced fracture; IV displaced; V nonunion). Hoenig 2022 (*Am J Sports Med*) — MRI grade correlates with return to sport.

**Risk classification (Boden & Osbahr 2000, *JAAOS*):**
- **Low-risk:** posteromedial tibia, fibula, 2nd–4th metatarsal shafts, calcaneus, compression-side femoral shaft. Relative rest, gradual progression.
- **High-risk:** femoral neck tension-side, patella, anterior tibial cortex, medial malleolus, talus, tarsal navicular, proximal 5th metatarsal (Jones), great toe sesamoids, **sacrum**. Urgent imaging; often non-weight-bearing or surgical.

**RED-S/Triad:** Mountjoy 2014 IOC consensus (*BJSM*) — any female athlete with BSI should be screened for low energy availability, menstrual dysfunction, low BMD; the same applies to males with multiple or atypical BSIs. Tenforde 2018 (*Am J Sports Med*) — low BMD in male athletes is associated with trabecular-site BSIs (sacrum, femoral neck, pubis).

### Specific High-Risk Stress Fractures (must-know for coaches)

**Femoral neck stress fracture (FNSF):**
- ~3–5% of sport-related stress fractures (Robertson & Wood 2017, *Sports Med Int Open*). Femoral neck force reaches 3–5× body weight in running.
- Insidious **groin pain**, worse weight-bearing, painful internal rotation, positive single-leg hop, often night pain.
- **Fullerton & Snowdy classification (1988, *Am J Sports Med*):** Type I tension-side (superior, high-risk, usually surgical due to displacement risk and AVN); Type II compression-side (inferior, conservative if undisplaced); Type III displaced (orthopedic emergency).
- "Groin strain" failing to improve in 2–3 weeks → MRI.
- Return to competition typically 16–24 weeks; displaced fractures have markedly worse outcomes.

**Sacral stress fracture (Fredericson 2003, *Phys Sportsmed*; Major & Helms 2000, *AJR*):**
- Often misdiagnosed: Major & Helms — all 4 long-distance runners had been treated for "disk disease" before correct diagnosis.
- Unilateral low-back/buttock/posterior pelvic pain with weight-bearing; tender over sacral ala, positive single-leg hop, positive FABER.
- 9 of 12 women in Fredericson's series were amenorrhoeic — strong RED-S association.
- MRI gold standard (X-ray usually negative). Beit Ner 2022 (*Eur Spine J*): "high percentage of lower bone mineral density can blur the lines between fatigue fractures and insufficiency fractures."
- Return to gradual running ~8 weeks; full activity 12–14 weeks.

**Anterior tibial cortex stress fracture ("dreaded black line"):**
- Tension-side, hypovascular; high non-union and complete-fracture risk.
- ~4–5% of tibial stress fractures (Al-Janabi 2023, *J Belg Soc Radiol*); predominantly jumping/leaping athletes (basketball, ballet, hurdling) but occurs in distance runners.
- Focal anterior mid-tibial point tenderness, persistent, failing 4+ weeks of rest.
- Orava 1991 (*Int J Sports Med*): with conservative management alone, only ~40% returned to full activity; many require IM nailing or anterior tension-band plating.

**Other high-risk sites coaches should flag:** Tarsal navicular ("N-spot" tenderness, dorsal midfoot, runners/sprinters); proximal 5th metatarsal (Jones zone); medial malleolus.

**Authoritative references:** Warden, Davis & Fredericson 2014 (*JOSPT*) — "Management and prevention of bone stress injuries in long-distance runners"; Bennell 1996a,b (*Am J Sports Med*); Hoenig 2023 (*BJSM*) return-to-sport meta-analysis.

### Hamstring Strain vs. Proximal Hamstring Tendinopathy (PHT)
**Acute strain (Type I, sprint-type):** sudden onset during high-speed running, "pop," pain at biceps femoris long-head proximal MTJ, bruising. Type II (lengthening-type) — slower onset, deeper semimembranosus.
**PHT:** insidious deep buttock/sit-bone pain, worse with sitting and hills, tender at ischial tuberosity. Common in distance runners; often missed/mislabeled.
**Differential for posterior thigh/buttock pain:** lumbar referral, sciatic nerve/deep gluteal syndrome, ischiogluteal bursitis, partial proximal hamstring avulsion (urgent MRI; complete avulsions with >2 cm retraction may need surgical repair).
**Return-to-sport for acute strain:** British Athletics staged protocol; MRI grade and time-to-walk-pain-free more reliable than time alone (Askling H-test).

### Calf Strain
Acute pain mid-belly of medial gastrocnemius ("tennis leg") or soleus during push-off; sharper for gastroc, more diffuse cramping/stiffness for soleus. Soleus strains common in distance runners — pain on bent-knee calf raise.
**Differential:** Achilles partial rupture (proximal), **DVT (urgent — calf swelling, redness, asymmetry; Wells score, same-day referral)**, Baker's cyst rupture, plantaris rupture.

### Hip Flexor Strain / Iliopsoas Tendinopathy
Anterior groin/hip pain with hip flexion. Snapping iliopsoas may click audibly.
**Differential:** Femoroacetabular impingement, labral tear, adductor strain, sports hernia, **femoral neck stress fracture** (must exclude with persistent groin pain).
**Chain:** Hip flexor tightness reciprocally inhibits gluteus maximus → reduced hip extension → lumbar compensation (Janda's "lower crossed syndrome" framework — clinically useful, not RCT-validated as a unified syndrome).

### Piriformis Syndrome / Deep Gluteal Syndrome
Deep buttock pain with sciatic-like radiation, worse sitting. "Piriformis syndrome" is now a subset of **deep gluteal syndrome** — sciatic entrapment from piriformis, obturator internus/gemelli, hamstring origin, or fibrous bands (Martin 2015, *Arthroscopy*).
**Differential:** Lumbar radiculopathy (positive slump/SLR, dermatomal), SI joint dysfunction, PHT, ischiogluteal bursitis, **sacral stress fracture**.
**Red flag:** Bilateral leg symptoms, saddle anesthesia, bladder/bowel changes → **cauda equina, emergency**.

### Lower Back Pain in Runners
**Prevalence:** ~14% point prevalence in middle/long-distance runners (Maselli 2020, *BMC Musculoskelet Disord*).
**Mechanism:** Repetitive compressive/rotational load; hip flexor tightness; weak deep core; reduced hip extension; SI stress.
**Red flags (any back pain):** Saddle anesthesia, bladder/bowel dysfunction, bilateral leg weakness (cauda equina); night pain with weight loss/cancer history (malignancy); fever (infection); trauma in older athlete (fracture).

### Chronic Exertional Compartment Syndrome (CECS)
Predictable crampy/tight pain in a leg compartment building over ~10–30 minutes of running, forcing cessation; resolves within ~15–30 min. May have paresthesia, foot drop (anterior), or weakness. Diagnosis by intracompartmental pressure (Pedowitz criteria). **Does not respond to load management** — surgical fasciotomy is often the only definitive treatment. Coach should refer rather than chase load tweaks.

## CYCLING INJURIES

### Anterior Knee Pain
Most common cycling overuse complaint (du Toit 2020 SAFER XIII: 14.2% of GOIs).
**PFPS in cyclists:** Risk factors: saddle too low, too far forward; cleats too far back; oversized gearing/low cadence; weak/inhibited VMO; weak gluteals (drives knee valgus and lateral patellar maltracking); excessive Q-angle.
**Patellar tendinopathy in cyclists:** more common in track/sprint disciplines; high-force, low-cadence; tender at inferior patellar pole.
**Quadriceps tendinopathy:** less common; tender at superior pole.
**Bike-fit response:** Raise saddle; move saddle back; move cleats forward; spin higher cadence.

### Lateral Knee Pain (ITBS in cyclists)
Mechanism differs from running: saddle too high or too far back drives minimum knee flexion <30°, keeping ITB in compression zone (Farrell 2003; Asplund & St Pierre 2004). Also: insufficient cleat float, varus tracking, wide stance.
**Fix:** Lower saddle, move forward, increase float, consider varus/valgus wedges.

### Medial Knee Pain
**Pes anserinus tendinopathy/bursitis:** tenderness ~2–5 cm distal-medial to joint line. Driven by knees collapsing medially — wide stance, cleats toe-in, weak hip abductors.
**Fix:** Pedal spacers/widen stance, realign cleats, hip strengthening.

### Posterior Knee Pain
Knee over-extension at bottom of stroke. Saddle too high or too far back → hamstring/popliteus overload, posterior capsule strain.
**Fix:** Lower saddle; target 25–30° knee flexion at bottom dead center (Bini 2011 systematic review; Holliday 2021).
**Differential:** Baker's cyst (persistent, palpable, refer for imaging), popliteus tendinopathy, hamstring tendinopathy.

### Lower Back Pain in Cyclists
**Most common overuse complaint in road cyclists** (Clarsen 2010: 45% of overuse injuries; prevalence 30–60%).
**Mechanism:** Sustained lumbar flexion; tight hip flexors holding pelvis in anterior tilt; weak lumbar extensors and deep core; reach too long, drop too aggressive (especially in TT/aero); time-trial and triathlon aero positions worst-affected.
**Fix:** Shorter stem/raise bars; hip flexor mobility; anti-extension core work (planks, dead bugs); gradual aero adaptation.
**Red flags:** Same as running — neuro signs → urgent referral.

### Neck and Upper-Back Pain
Up to ~49% of recreational cyclists (Wilber 1995, *Int J Sports Med*).
**Mechanism:** Sustained cervical extension; weak deep cervical flexors; thoracic kyphosis amplifies needed cervical extension; helmet weight; long reach.
**Fix:** Reduce reach/drop, vary hand position, cervical/thoracic mobility, deep neck flexor strengthening.

### Hand Numbness / Cyclist's Palsy
**Ulnar (handlebar palsy):** numbness/weakness in ring and little fingers; compression of ulnar nerve in Guyon's canal at the hypothenar eminence. Common with prolonged grip in drops/hoods, wrist extension/ulnar deviation, road vibration. Rauch 2016 — ulnar nerve closer to hamate with wrist extended/ulnar-deviated. Slane 2011 — glove type/thickness and hand position influence pressure.
**Median (carpal-tunnel pattern):** thumb, index, middle finger numbness; wrist hyperextension on bars.
**Fix:** Vary hand position, padded gloves and bar tape, neutral wrist, reduce hand-loading (level saddle, raise bars).
**Red flag:** Symptoms persisting >2 weeks off bike, motor weakness → refer (rare cases need surgical release).

### Saddle / Perineal Issues
**Saddle sores:** folliculitis, furuncles, abrasions, subcutaneous nodules — friction, sweat, prolonged sitting. Hygiene, chamois cream, well-fitting shorts, saddle change.
**Pudendal nerve compression / pudendal neuralgia ("cyclist's syndrome"):** perineal numbness, burning, sexual dysfunction (penile/scrotal numbness, ED in men; vulvar pain in women). Durante 2010 (peer-reviewed estimate cited in PMC2989401): **7–8% of cyclists on long-distance multi-day rides experience pudendal neuralgia**.
**Mechanism:** Compression of pudendal nerve in Alcock's canal between saddle nose and pubic ramus; worsened by forward-leaning aero posture, narrow saddle, upward-tilted nose, saddle too high (causes rocking).
**Fix:** Nose-down or cut-out/split-nose saddle (e.g., split-nose designs); level or slight nose-down tilt; saddle width matched to sit-bone width; lower saddle if rocking; periodic standing.
**Red flag:** Persistent numbness >1 week off bike, motor weakness, urinary/bowel symptoms → urgent urology/neurology.

### Achilles Issues from Cleat Position
Cleats too far forward (under the metatarsal heads or beyond) → increased calf and Achilles load → tendinopathy, cramping. Mid-foot cleat positioning (under base of metatarsals or slightly behind) reduces calf demand. Cleat rotational alignment also matters — fixed cleats forcing rotation drive knee pain.

### Iliac Artery Endofibrosis (IAE)
Rare (global prevalence ~0.01%) but commonly misdiagnosed and clinically critical. Per Schep (cited in PMC8147885 and PMC11479865), **IAE accounts for up to ~20% of all overuse leg injuries in professional cyclists** (not 20% of the population). A *Canadian Family Physician* (2016) review estimates 10–20% of elite amateur or professional cyclists <40 years are affected, ~80% male. Cyclists with IAE had cycled an average of ~120,000 km prior to symptom onset, >14,500 km/year (Peach G, Schep G, et al. 2012, *Eur J Vasc Endovasc Surg* systematic review). Left side most common (~90% external iliac artery); also affects triathletes and runners.
**Presentation:** Unilateral exertional pain, cramping, weakness, or "loss of power" in thigh, calf, or buttock at near-maximal effort. Resolves within minutes of stopping. Asymptomatic at rest. Triggered by hip flexion in aero position. Average 3-year diagnostic delay.
**Diagnosis:** Pre/post-exercise ankle-brachial index, duplex ultrasound during/after maximal effort, MR or CT angiography (often performed with hip flexion).
**Red flag pattern for the coach:** Endurance cyclist (especially high-lifetime-mileage male) with reproducible unilateral high-intensity-only leg pain or power loss → vascular sports medicine referral.

### Hip Flexor / Iliopsoas Tightness in Cyclists
Sustained ~90° hip flexion → adaptive shortening; combined with reciprocal gluteal inhibition → anterior pelvic tilt, low back overload, reduced running stride length (relevant in tri).

## SWIMMING INJURIES

### Swimmer's Shoulder (umbrella term)
**Encompasses:** subacromial impingement, rotator cuff tendinopathy (especially supraspinatus), long-head biceps tendinopathy, SLAP lesions, scapular dyskinesis, multidirectional instability (Hill 2015; Tovin 2006).
**Prevalence:** 40–91% of competitive swimmers (definition-dependent).
**Volume context:** Elite swimmers 60,000–80,000 m/week; each shoulder ~30,000 revolutions/week (Heinlein & Cosgarea 2010).
**Mechanism:** Repetitive overhead in internal rotation/adduction during pull-through → impingement of supraspinatus and long head of biceps under coracoacromial arch. Imbalances: overdeveloped pectoralis major and latissimus → anterior humeral head translation; weak/late serratus anterior and lower trapezius → scapular dyskinesis → reduced subacromial space.
**Chain — thoracic spine:** Thoracic kyphosis and forward head posture reduce scapular upward rotation and posterior tilt → reduced subacromial space → impingement (Kibler 2013, *BJSM*; Kebaetse 1999). Thoracic mobility work is foundational.
**Stroke factors (coachable):** Early hand entry crossing midline, dropped elbow in pull, insufficient body roll (forces shoulder into impingement position).
**Differential for shoulder pain in swimmers:**
- **Subacromial impingement / RC tendinopathy:** painful arc 60–120°, positive Hawkins/Neer, weakness on empty-can
- **Long-head biceps tendinopathy:** anterior shoulder pain, tender bicipital groove, positive Speed's/Yergason's
- **SLAP lesion:** deep clicking/catching, positive O'Brien's; many "incidentaloma" findings on MRI in athletes >40 (Bennett 2009)
- **Multidirectional instability:** sulcus sign, apprehension, sensation of looseness; up to 62% of swimmers have generalized laxity
- **Cervical referral (C5–C6):** pain radiating below elbow, neuro signs, reproduced by Spurling's
- **AC joint pathology:** focal AC tenderness, positive cross-body adduction
- **Suprascapular neuropathy:** isolated infraspinatus wasting (paraglenoid notch entrapment)
**Red flags:** Night pain unrelieved by position change, true weakness (not pain-limited) → consider full-thickness tear, urgent imaging. Trauma → labral/RC tear.

### Lower Back Issues in Swimmers
Hyperextension during streamline/dolphin kick/butterfly; lumbar facet pain and disc strain in adults. Less common in triathletes (freestyle-dominant, minimal dolphin kick).

### Neck Issues in Swimmers
**Breathing-side asymmetry:** Unilateral breathing → asymmetric cervical rotation load → upper trap/levator scapulae tension, facet irritation. Bilateral breathing reduces asymmetry.

### Breaststroker's Knee
Medial knee pain from valgus-loaded whip kick; MCL stress, medial plica, pes anserinus. Mostly irrelevant to triathlon (freestyle-dominant) but worth mentioning if athletes do significant breaststroke training.

## CROSS-CUTTING BIOMECHANICAL CHAINS

### Hip/Pelvic Stability
**The dominant proximal chain in lower-limb running injuries.** Weak/poorly recruited gluteus medius (and gluteus maximus) → contralateral pelvic drop in single-leg stance → femoral adduction and internal rotation → dynamic knee valgus → increased Q-angle, lateral patellar maltracking (PFPS), increased ITB tension (ITBS), increased medial foot loading (pronation cascade → MTSS, plantar fasciitis, posterior tib tendinopathy).
**Evidence:** Powers 2010 (*JOSPT*) — hip mechanics and knee injury; Lack 2015 systematic review (*BJSM*) — hip strengthening reduces PFPS pain.
**Coaching cue:** Gait retraining to reduce peak hip adduction (visual/auditory cueing, mirror gait) has the best evidence.

### Foot Mechanics
**Overpronation cascade:** Excessive/prolonged pronation → tibial internal rotation → patellar maltracking, medial knee stress, posterior tibial tendon overload, plantar fascia strain.
**Cavus (high-arched, rigid) foot:** Poor shock absorption → impact-related issues (stress fractures, lateral ankle sprains).
**Evidence caveat (contested):** Pronation-control shoe prescription is not RCT-supported (Knapik 2014, *Footwear Sci*; Ryan 2010 stability shoe RCT). Modern Nigg "preferred movement pathway" model emphasizes comfort-based selection. Coaches should not rigidly prescribe shoes based on static arch type.

### Thoracic Mobility (swimmers and cyclists especially)
Thoracic kyphosis/limited extension → reduced scapular upward rotation and posterior tilt → reduced subacromial space (swimmer's shoulder). In cyclists, reduced thoracic extension forces cervical hyperextension → neck pain. In runners, stiffness reduces arm swing and rotational efficiency.
**Response:** Daily thoracic extension mobility (foam roller, peanut, open-book rotations); don't prescribe rotator cuff strengthening without addressing thoracic posture.

### Ankle Dorsiflexion
**Threshold:** ≥10° weight-bearing DF (knee-to-wall test, ≥5 cm from wall). Below this:
- Increased knee valgus during squatting/landing (Mauntel 2013; Frontiers 2025 review)
- Higher peak GRF, reduced knee flexion at landing → patellofemoral and patellar tendon load
- Compensatory pronation → MTSS, plantar fasciitis cascade
- In cycling: forces toe-down pedaling, increased Achilles/calf load
**Causes:** Calf-soleus stiffness, post-sprain capsular restriction (restricted posterior talar glide), bony block.

### Muscle Inhibition / Reciprocal Patterns
**Hip flexor tightness ↔ gluteal inhibition (Janda "lower crossed syndrome"):** prolonged sitting/cycling shortens iliopsoas; reciprocally inhibits glute max → reduced hip extension → lumbar extension compensates → lumbar facet irritation. Clinically useful framework, not RCT-validated as a unified syndrome.
**Pain inhibition:** Knee or shoulder pain itself rapidly inhibits VMO/serratus anterior — "weakness" found in the clinic may be cause OR effect.

### Strength Deficits and Specific Injury Links
- **Weak hip abductors:** ITBS, PFPS, MTSS — strong evidence
- **Weak hip external rotators:** PFPS, ITBS, valgus collapse — moderate
- **Weak posterior chain (glute max, hamstrings):** LBP, hamstring strain recurrence, PHT — moderate
- **Weak deep core (multifidus, TVA):** LBP — moderate; broader "core stability" claims contested
- **Weak rotator cuff (especially infraspinatus):** swimmer's shoulder — moderate to strong
- **Weak scapular stabilizers (serratus anterior, lower trap):** swimmer's shoulder, neck pain — strong observational

## BIKE-FIT INJURY MAPPING

| Symptom Location | Likely Fit Issue | Adjustment |
|---|---|---|
| Anterior knee | Saddle too low/forward; cleats too far back; oversized gear | Raise saddle; move back; cleats forward; spin higher |
| Posterior knee | Saddle too high/back; cleat too far forward | Lower saddle; move forward |
| Lateral knee (ITB) | Saddle too high or too far back; insufficient float | Lower saddle; add float; varus wedge |
| Medial knee (pes anserinus) | Stance too narrow; cleats toe-in; weak abductors | Pedal spacers; cleat realignment; hip work |
| Lower back | Reach too long; drop too aggressive; tight hip flexors | Shorter stem/raise bars; hip flexor mobility |
| Neck/upper trap | Reach too long; thoracic kyphosis | Reduce reach/drop; mobility; vary hand position |
| Hands/ulnar | Too much weight on hands (saddle nose-down, bars too low/far) | Level saddle; raise bars; padded gloves; vary position |
| Perineum | Saddle nose-up; wrong width; saddle too high | Level or nose-down; sit-bone-matched width; cut-out/split-nose; lower if rocking |
| Achilles/calf | Cleats too far forward | Move cleats back toward mid-foot |
| Anterior groin (persistent) | Saddle too high/forward — but rule out FNSF | Lower saddle; refer if persistent |

**Target knee flexion at bottom dead center: 25–30°** (Bini 2011 systematic review).

## RED FLAGS — IMMEDIATE / SAME-WEEK MEDICAL REFERRAL

**Neurological:**
- Saddle anesthesia, bladder/bowel changes, bilateral leg weakness → **cauda equina syndrome, emergency**
- New foot drop, progressive weakness, dermatomal sensory loss

**Bone:**
- Focal point tenderness in groin (FNSF), anterior mid-tibia, sacrum, navicular ("N-spot"), proximal 5th metatarsal → MRI
- Night pain at rest, especially with weight loss/cancer history
- Female athlete with stress fracture + amenorrhea/disordered eating → RED-S screen

**Cardiovascular:**
- Exertional chest pain, syncope, palpitations → urgent cardiology
- Unilateral calf swelling/redness/warmth → DVT same-day workup
- Endurance cyclist with reproducible unilateral max-effort leg pain → IAE workup

**Joint:**
- Locking, true giving way, significant effusion → internal derangement
- Sudden "pop" + inability to plantarflex → Achilles rupture (Thompson test)
- Acute hamstring with palpable defect/severe weakness → consider proximal avulsion

**Persistent/refractory:**
- Musculoskeletal pain not improving with 4–6 weeks of appropriate load management
- Bilateral heel pain in young male → seronegative spondyloarthropathy screen
- Pain at rest, night pain, systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss)

## "LOOKS LIKE X, OFTEN IS Y" — COMMON MISDIAGNOSES

- **"Shin splints" not improving** → **tibial stress fracture or CECS** (focal tenderness, crescendo, night pain shift probability).
- **"Sciatica" in a female runner** → **sacral stress fracture**, especially with amenorrhea (Major & Helms 2000: 4/4 misdiagnosed as disc disease).
- **"Hamstring strain" that won't resolve** → **proximal hamstring tendinopathy** (chronic, worse with sitting).
- **"Groin strain" >2–3 weeks** → **femoral neck stress fracture** until proven otherwise.
- **"IT band tightness" requiring more rolling** → **hip abductor weakness/endurance deficit** (compression, not friction).
- **"Plantar fasciitis"** with diffuse heel pain → **calcaneal stress fracture** (squeeze test) or **Baxter's nerve entrapment**.
- **"Patellar tendinopathy" in a cyclist** → often **PFPS or fat pad impingement**.
- **"Cramping/lactic" at max effort, unilateral, in a strong cyclist** → **iliac artery endofibrosis** (avg 3-year diagnostic delay).
- **"Overtraining" with focal bone pain** → bone stress injury; screen for RED-S in females.
- **"Shoulder impingement" with looseness/apprehension** → **multidirectional instability** (treating impingement without addressing instability fails).
- **"Carpal tunnel" in a cyclist** → median nerve compression at the wrist from bars — reversible with fit changes.
- **"Trochanteric bursitis"** → almost always **gluteal tendinopathy** (current term: greater trochanteric pain syndrome).

## CONTESTED EVIDENCE — WHERE COACHES SHOULD HEDGE

- **Foot-strike pattern as injury risk:** No clear evidence heel-strike vs. mid-foot vs. fore-foot strike independently predicts injury. Forefoot striking shifts load from knee to Achilles/calf — changes injury *location*, not necessarily incidence (Hamill & Gruber 2017, *J Sport Health Sci*).
- **Pronation-based shoe prescription:** Not RCT-supported (Knapik 2014; Ryan 2010). Comfort-based selection preferred.
- **Static stretching for prevention:** Does not robustly reduce injury and may transiently impair performance. By contrast, Lauersen 2014 (*BJSM*) meta-analysis of 26,610 participants found that **"strength training reduced sports injuries to less than 1/3 and overuse injuries could be almost halved"** (overuse RR 0.527, 95% CI 0.373–0.746).
- **"Core stability" as panacea:** Specific deep-core work has moderate evidence for LBP; broader injury-prevention claims are weaker.
- **Cadence "180 spm rule":** Heiderscheit 2011 (*Med Sci Sports Exerc*) — 5–10% relative cadence increase reduces knee and hip loading. The 180 absolute is not magical; *relative* increase matters. Forcing 180 spm in a 168 spm runner increases metabolic cost without proportional benefit.
- **Foam rolling/massage tissue change:** Acute ROM and pain benefits supported; structural-tissue-change claims overstated.

## Recommendations (for the coaching agent's decision behavior)

**Tier 1 — Suspect, load-manage, monitor:**
Overuse presentations with no red flags (mild PFPS; MTSS without focal tenderness; mid-portion Achilles; plantar fasciopathy; lateral knee pain consistent with ITBS; recreational LBP): reduce running volume 30–50% for 1–2 weeks, maintain cross-training in pain-free disciplines, address the most likely upstream factor (cadence increase, hip strengthening, ankle DF mobility, bike-fit adjustment), reassess at 2 weeks.

**Tier 2 — Refer within 1–2 weeks if:**
- No improvement after 2 weeks of conservative load management
- Pain at rest or sleep
- Asymmetry developing in gait
- New mechanical symptoms (clicking, locking, snapping with pain)

**Tier 3 — Same-week referral / stop training:**
- Any red flag listed above
- Suspected bone stress injury (focal tenderness, crescendo pain)
- Suspected high-risk site (groin, anterior tibia, sacrum, navicular, 5th MT base)
- Neurological signs (saddle anesthesia, foot drop, bowel/bladder)
- Cardiovascular symptoms or unilateral cyclist's claudication
- Female with amenorrhea + bone pain
- Suspected Achilles or hamstring rupture

**Benchmarks that should change the plan:**
- Pain worsening over consecutive sessions despite load reduction → escalate tier
- Night pain emerging → escalate
- Pain bilaterally symmetric and persisting → systemic workup considered
- Female athlete with stress reaction + menstrual disturbance → RED-S referral regardless of severity
- Unilateral high-effort-only leg symptoms in a high-mileage cyclist → vascular referral, do not load-manage

**The coach should never:**
- Override pain that worsens with continued training
- Prescribe specific rehabilitation exercises for a diagnosed pathology (refer to physiotherapy)
- Make formal diagnoses; use "consistent with" framing
- Dismiss persistent unilateral pain as "just tightness"
- Continue full training load through a suspected stress reaction

## Caveats

- Prevalence figures vary considerably across studies due to differences in injury definition, study population (elite vs. recreational vs. novice), and surveillance method. Triathlon-specific overuse prevalence is best anchored at 50–75% (Rhind 2022) with 56% average prevalence reported in iron-distance athletes (Andersen/Clarsen 2013, *BJSM*).
- Many biomechanical "cascades" here are clinically observed and physiologically plausible but not all are validated by prospective intervention trials. Strongest evidence: hip-strength → ITBS/PFPS; eccentric loading for mid-portion Achilles; cadence increase for knee load; strength training for general overuse prevention (Lauersen 2014). Weaker/contested: "lower crossed syndrome," static pronation-based footwear prescription, footstrike-pattern claims.
- This is a coach's reference for triage and escalation, not a diagnostic tool. Sports medicine physicians and physiotherapists make diagnoses; the coach's role is suspecting them, managing load, and knowing when to escalate.
- Out of scope: pediatric/adolescent injuries (apophysitis, Osgood-Schlatter, spondylolysis), post-surgical rehabilitation specifics, detailed exercise prescription, general health/cardiac/metabolic conditions.
- Evolving consensus: ITB compression (not friction) — Fairclough 2006; biceps tenodesis often preferred over SLAP repair in older athletes; RED-S (Mountjoy 2014 IOC, updated 2018/2023) supersedes the narrower Female Athlete Triad framework; greater trochanteric pain syndrome replaces "trochanteric bursitis."
